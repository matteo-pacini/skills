# Equipment — machine types and how they change the balance

The composition targets in this skill are calibrated for a mix **churned in a batch
freezer and served from a cabinet at −12 to −14 °C**. Other home machines work
differently enough that you should nudge the targets — not just the method — before
handing the user a recipe. If the user names a machine, read this and apply its column.

| Machine | How it works | Overrun | Target nudges vs default | Process notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Batch freezer / compressor** (Lello, Musso, self-refrigerating) | Churns + freezes the liquid mix together | Med (25–35%) | **Use bands as-is** — this is the calibration baseline | Pre-chill per manual; 20–40 min churn. |
| **Pre-freeze bowl** (Cuisinart, KitchenAid bowl) | A pre-frozen bowl chills a churning liquid; finite cold | Low–med (20–30%) | PAC **mid-band**; don't over-soften or it won't set before the bowl warms | Freeze bowl ≥24 h; chill mix to fridge temp first; churn fast (18–25 min) before the bowl exhausts. |
| **Ninja Creami** | Freeze the mix **solid** 24 h, then shave it to a paste | **Very low (~0–10%)** | **PAC to the LOW end; TS to the HIGH end (40–42 / 32–33); stabiliser high (~0.4%); body via maltodextrin/low-DE syrup, not extra dextrose** | Freeze flat & level 24 h; run the Gelato/Sorbet program; **re-spin 1–2×; add a splash of milk if crumbly**; refreeze leftovers and re-spin. |
| **Pacojet** | Shaves a deep-frozen (−20 to −22 °C) beaker | Low unless aerated | PAC low–mid; standard TS | Freeze beaker ≥24 h at −20 °C; pacotize 1–2×; smooth even at modest solids. |
| **No machine / still-freezing** | Freeze in a tub stirring every 30–45 min, or freeze-then-blitz | ~0 | **PAC high + stabiliser high + TS high** to fight big crystals; accept it's the weakest texture | Stir every 30–40 min for 3–4 h, or freeze to slush and blitz in a processor. |

## The Ninja Creami is the important exception
The Creami **inverts two of this skill's defaults**, so don't just hand a cabinet recipe to a Creami user:

1. It needs the mix to **freeze into a solid homogeneous block**, then makes it creamy by *shaving*, not by anti-freeze. A high-PAC recipe (balanced soft at −18 °C) freezes mushy and shaves poorly. So a Creami wants **PAC at the LOW end of the band** — the opposite of the "raise PAC for a home −18 °C freezer / temper before serving" advice that applies to scoop-from-the-tub gelato.
2. Shaving is brutal on texture, so push **total solids to the top of the band** and **stabiliser to the high end**, and build body with maltodextrin/low-DE syrup rather than monosaccharides. **Re-spin** is a normal step, not a failure — the first spin is often powdery; re-spin once or twice, adding a tablespoon of milk if it stays crumbly.

## Overrun (air)
Gelato is intentionally **low-overrun** (denser) than American ice cream (~25–35% vs
50–100%); home machines push it lower still, and the Creami/Pacojet are near-zero.
Low overrun eats colder and melts faster — temper accordingly — and don't over-churn
chasing volume or you'll get butter (high-fat mixes) or a coarse texture. Set the
user's expectation: dense is correct for gelato.

## Sizing the batch to the machine
Don't blindly default to 1000 g. Fill a churn machine to **50–60%** of its bowl (mixes
expand). A **Ninja Creami pint holds ~700 g max**; a 1.5 L Cuisinart wants ~700–900 g
of mix. To convert a volume request to grams, mix density ≈ **1.07 g/mL** (so 1.5 L ≈
1600 g of mix), and remember the churned volume is ~30% larger than the mix.
